# Nadi Suddhi Pranayama (Alternate Nostril Breathing)

*Nadi* (Sanskrit) — channel, river (in yogic anatomy: the subtle energy channels through which prana flows); *Suddhi* — purification, cleansing. Nadi Suddhi purifies the energy channels by alternately directing breath through the left and right nostrils.

This is one of the most foundational breath practices in therapeutic yoga because it is simple, scalable, and usually well tolerated.

**Evidence note:** External evidence for alternate-nostril breathing is strongest for short-term autonomic and blood-pressure outcomes, especially when the breath is slow and relaxed. Condition-specific claims across many diseases should be treated as program-level yoga-therapy applications rather than as single-practice proof.

---

## 1. Technique

**Starting position (Sthiti):** Sit comfortably in [Vajrasana](P0154-vajrasana.md), [Padmasana](P0166-padmasana.md), or [Sukhasana](P0167-sukhasana.md), with spine erect. If needed, sit on a chair with feet flat on the floor.

**Hand position:** Use [Nasika Mudra](P0168-nasika-mudra.md) — fold the index and middle fingers of the right hand. The right thumb closes the right nostril; the ring and little fingers close the left nostril. Elbows rest on the abdomen; shoulders loose and relaxed.

**Process (one round):**
1. Close the right nostril with the right thumb. **Inhale slowly** through the left nostril, allowing the breath to be full, smooth, and effortless.
2. At the peak of inhalation, enjoy the natural pause (Kevala Kumbhaka — the spontaneous, effortless breath retention at the turn). Do not force this.
3. Close the left nostril with the ring and little fingers. Open the right nostril by releasing the thumb. **Exhale slowly** through the right nostril, completely and without strain.
4. Enjoy the natural pause at the end of exhalation.
5. **Inhale through the right nostril** (right still open).
6. At the peak, enjoy Kevala Kumbhaka.
7. Close the right nostril with the thumb. Open the left nostril. **Exhale through the left nostril**.
8. This completes one round.

**Rounds in IAYT protocols:** 9 rounds (approximately 2–3 minutes). Some protocols extend to 18 or 27 rounds for specific conditions.

**Ratio:** No forced ratio is used in the therapeutic context — the breath is slow, natural, and un-strained. The advanced practice involves Puraka (inhalation), Kumbhaka (retention), and Recaka (exhalation) in specific ratios (e.g. 1:4:2), but this is introduced gradually under guidance.

**Face:** Relaxed, gently smiling. No forceful nostril movements.

## 2. Anatomical Activation

### Primary structures engaged

**Nasal passages and nasal turbinates** — alternating airflow through left and right nostrils. The turbinates (scroll-like bony structures lined with mucous membrane) regulate airflow temperature, humidity, and direction. Each nostril connects through distinct neural pathways to different parts of the brain.

**Nasal mucosa and olfactory epithelium** — rich in nerve endings connected to the olfactory bulb and, through it, to the limbic system (emotion regulation), hypothalamus (autonomic control), and prefrontal cortex.

**Diaphragm** — slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing is the primary respiratory mechanism. The diaphragm descends fully on inhalation (abdominal expansion), creating negative intrathoracic pressure that draws air in without effort.

**Intercostal muscles** — gentle engagement for lateral thoracic expansion during full inhalation.

**Nasika Mudra hand (right)** — the alternating thumb/ring-little-finger closure creates the alternating airflow pattern.

### Nervous system structures

**Autonomic nervous system:** In yogic physiology, the left nostril is associated with Ida (cooling, calming) and the right nostril with Pingala (activating, warming). Modern breath research suggests that slow, paced breathing can influence autonomic regulation, but the exact hemisphere-autonomic mapping should be presented cautiously in public-facing material.

**Vagus nerve (Cranial Nerve X):** Slow diaphragmatic breathing — the foundation of Nadi Suddhi — directly stimulates vagal afferents in the diaphragm and lungs. Increased vagal tone is the primary parasympathetic pathway.

**Hypothalamus:** The olfactory-limbic pathway provides direct input to the hypothalamus, the master regulator of the autonomic nervous system, HPA axis, and metabolic function.

## 3. Physiological Effects

### Autonomic nervous system balance — the central mechanism

The clearest modern interpretation of Nadi Suddhi is that it is a **slow, paced breathing practice** with alternating nostril control. That combination may help reduce respiratory rate, improve breath awareness, and support calmer autonomic regulation.

Current evidence is most comfortable supporting short-term changes in measures such as blood pressure, heart rate, and heart-rate variability. Broader claims about cortisol, insulin sensitivity, or disease-specific mechanisms should be treated as plausible extensions of stress-regulation theory rather than as settled single-practice facts.

### Respiratory normalisation

Slow breathing (typically 4–6 breaths per minute during Nadi Suddhi, compared to the normal 12–16) has measurable effects:
- Reduces respiratory rate and tidal volume demand
- Increases carbon dioxide tolerance (CO₂ is the primary driver of the urge to breathe; raising tolerance allows slower, deeper breaths)
- Improves oxygen extraction efficiency per breath
- Reduces work of breathing in asthma and COPD

### Nasal passage health

Alternating airflow keeps both nasal passages clear and well-circulated, reducing the tendency to chronic nasal congestion, allergic rhinitis, and sinusitis. The turbinates maintain optimal temperature and humidity for inspired air, protecting the lower respiratory tract.

## 4. Kosha Level

**Primary:** Pranamaya Kosha — the direct regulation of prana (life force) through the breath channels. In yogic anatomy, the Ida (left/lunar/parasympathetic) and Pingala (right/solar/sympathetic) nadis are balanced through Nadi Suddhi. The goal is Sushumna awakening — the balanced central channel — prerequisite for meditative states.

**Secondary:** Manomaya Kosha — the calming of the mind through breath regulation. In the S-VYASA framework, Nadi Suddhi is used as a practical way to slow the breath, reduce agitation, and prepare for deeper relaxation or meditation.

The Pranamaya and Manomaya koshas are directly linked in the yoga therapy model: restoring pranic balance through Nadi Suddhi quiets mental turbulence, creating the conditions for deeper meditation.

## 6. Contraindications

- **Acute nasal congestion** — if both nostrils are blocked, the alternate pattern cannot be established. Practice Neti (Jala Neti) first to clear passages, then attempt Nadi Suddhi.
- **Forced Kumbhaka (breath retention)** with Bandhas — the advanced retention practices are contraindicated in hypertension, IHD, pregnancy, glaucoma, and epilepsy. The basic Nadi Suddhi without forced retention is safe for virtually all conditions.
- **No absolute contraindications for basic Nadi Suddhi** — it is the most universally safe pranayama in the IAYT repertoire.

## 7. Variations & Progressions

**Most basic (Sukha Pranayama / Dirgha Svasana):** Both nostrils open, simple slow deep breathing. This is often the starting point before formal alternate-nostril practice.

**Candranuloma-Viloma:** Inhale and exhale through the left nostril only (right closed throughout). Cooling, calming, parasympathetic dominant. Indicated for hypertension, anxiety, insomnia. 27 rounds, four times daily for significant therapeutic benefit.

**Suryanuloma-Viloma:** Inhale and exhale through the right nostril only. Warming, stimulating, sympathetic activating. Indicated for obesity and weight reduction (raises metabolic rate). Also useful for low energy states.

**Candra Bhedana:** Inhale left, exhale right. Cooling.

**Surya Bhedana:** Inhale right, exhale left. Warming.

**Full Nadi Suddhi (as described):** Inhale left, exhale right, inhale right, exhale left.

**Advanced with Kumbhaka and Bandhas:** Puraka (inhalation) : Kumbhaka (retention with Jalandhara and Moola Bandha) : Recaka (exhalation) in 1:4:2 ratio. Practised only under direct guidance of an experienced teacher. Contraindicated for beginners and in multiple medical conditions.
