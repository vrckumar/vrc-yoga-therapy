# Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation)

*Sūrya* (Sanskrit) — sun; *Namaskāra* — salutation, prostration. The Sun Salutation is a flowing 12-position dynamic sequence combining forward bends, backward bends, a lunge, a plank, and a prostration position in a single continuous cycle synchronised with the breath. Each round may also be linked with one of 12 Bīja (seed) mantras and the associated name of the Sun.

In IAYT, Surya Namaskar is used as a **dynamic conditioning and mobilisation sequence**, especially in vitality, obesity, and metabolic-support contexts.

**Evidence note:** External evidence for Surya Namaskar is strongest for general fitness, mobility, cardiorespiratory demand, and exercise-like metabolic effects. Public-facing claims should stay more cautious about endocrine, pancreatic, or disease-specific outcomes unless those are backed by broader program-level evidence rather than the sequence alone.

---

## 1. Technique

**Starting position (Sthiti):** Tāḍāsana. Stand erect with legs together. Begin with Praṇāmāsana — bring the palms together in Namaste Mudrā at the chest, eyes softly closed. The round begins with the utterance of the appropriate Bīja mantra and the name of the Sun God.

---

### The 12 Positions

**Position 1 — Hastautthānāsana** *(Inhale)*
Take the arms above the head while inhaling; bend the trunk backward. Biceps touch the ears, palms face forward. Open the chest fully; gaze upward and slightly back.
*Anatomical effect*: Thoracic extension; anterior chest opening; lung apex expansion; spinal axial traction.

**Position 2 — Pādahastāsana** *(Exhale)*
Bend the body forward while exhaling. Touch the forehead to the knees. Place the palms flat on the floor on either side of the feet. Knees remain straight throughout.
*Anatomical effect*: Hamstring stretch; spinal forward flexion; abdominal organ compression; mild cranial inversion.

**Position 3 — Aśvasañcālanāsana** *(Inhale)*
Inhale and kick the right leg back. The right knee may rest on the floor in the beginner version; the right toes are on the floor, right shin inclined backward. The left leg is perpendicular to the ground (knee directly above the foot). Push the pelvis forward and downward. Look upward, arch the spine.
*Anatomical effect*: Right hip flexor (iliopsoas) maximum stretch; left quadriceps strengthening; thoracic extension.

**Position 4 — Caturanga Daṇḍāsana** *(Exhale)*
Exhale and take the left leg also back, resting only on palms and toes. Keep the body in a straight line from head to toes, inclined at approximately 30° to the ground. Neck in line with the back.
*Anatomical effect*: Full core and shoulder isometric strengthening; plank position; bilateral hip-flexor and anterior body activation.

**Position 5 — Śaśāṅkāsana** *(Exhale and normal breathing)*
While inhaling, bend the legs at the knees and rest them on the floor; buttocks rest on the inside surface of the feet with the heels touching the sides of the hips, without altering the positions of the palms and toes. Exhale as the forehead rests on the floor. Relax in normal breathing.
*Anatomical effect*: Lumbar decompression; quadriceps and hip flexor release; adrenal quieting; spinal counterpose.

**Position 6 — Aṣṭāṅga Namaskāra** *(Exhale and Bāhya Kumbhaka — external breath retention)*
Exhale; without shifting the positions of hands and toes, glide the body forward and hold the breath externally. Eight points of the body rest on the ground: forehead, chest, both hands, both knees, and both toes. The buttocks are raised off the ground.
*Anatomical effect*: Spinal extension begins at the lower vertebrae; chest compressed; hip flexors and wrists loaded.

**Position 7 — Bhujaṅgāsana** *(Inhale)*
Inhale; raise the head and trunk, making the spine concave upward without lifting the position of the hands and feet. Arch the back as far as possible until the elbows are straight (or semi-straight). Keep the knees off the ground.
*Anatomical effect*: Thoracic spinal extension; chest opening; erector spinae strengthening; adrenal stimulation.

**Position 8 — Parvatāsana** *(Exhale)*
Exhale; raise the buttocks, push the head downward until the heels approach the ground without shifting the position of hands and feet. The body forms an inverted V.
*Anatomical effect*: Hamstring stretch; calf stretch; shoulder traction; mild inversion; Achilles tendon lengthening.

**Position 9 — Śaśāṅkāsana** *(Exhale and normal breathing)*
Same as position 5 — repeat the child's pose transition.
*Anatomical effect*: Second lumbar decompression; hip flexor release; transition phase.

**Position 10 — Aśvasañcālanāsana** *(Inhale)*
Inhale and bring the right leg forward between the two hands, so the right knee is above the right foot. Arch the back concave upward as in position 3, but now with the right leg forward.
*Anatomical effect*: Left hip flexor stretch (opposite side from position 3); bilateral balance of hip flexor lengthening over the complete round.

**Position 11 — Pādahastāsana** *(Exhale)*
Exhale and bring the left foot forward to meet the right foot. Bring the upper body down to touch the forehead to the knees, palms on floor, as in position 2.
*Anatomical effect*: Bilateral hamstring stretch; abdominal compression; cranial inversion.

**Position 12 — Hastautthānāsana** *(Inhale)*
Take the arms above the head while inhaling and bend the trunk backward, as in position 1.
Return to Praṇāmāsana on the exhale; then to Tāḍāsana Sthiti.
*Anatomical effect*: Thoracic extension; anterior chain re-opening after the sustained forward flexion.

This completes **one round**. A minimum of **3 rounds** constitutes a therapeutic practice session. In the Obesity protocol, progressive practice builds toward 12–24 rounds.

1. Inhale | 2. Exhale | 3. Inhale | 4. Exhale | 5. Exhale+Normal | 6. Exhale+Hold | 7. Inhale | 8. Exhale | 9. Exhale+Normal | 10. Inhale | 11. Exhale | 12. Inhale

**Note**: In the 10-position Sūryanamaskāra, positions 5 and 9 (Śaśāṅkāsana) are omitted.

## 2. Anatomical Activation — Position-by-Position Summary

Every major joint and muscle group in the body is addressed in one complete round:

| Body region | Positions primarily addressed |
|---|---|
| Spine — extension | 1, 3, 7, 10, 12 |
| Spine — flexion | 2, 5, 8, 9, 11 |
| Shoulders / chest | 1, 4, 6, 7, 12 |
| Hip flexors | 3, 10 |
| Hamstrings | 2, 8, 11 |
| Core / plank | 4, 6 |
| Wrists / arms | 4, 6, 7, 8 |
| Inversion / cranial | 2, 8, 11 |
| Relaxation / counterpose | 5, 9 |

The bilateral symmetry across 12 positions ensures that both hips are stretched (position 3 stretches the right hip; position 10 stretches the left) and both directions of spinal flexion and extension are fully loaded.

## 3. Physiological Effects

### Conditioning and whole-body mobilisation

Surya Namaskar behaves more like a **dynamic exercise sequence** than a single static posture. When performed repeatedly and at an appropriate pace, it can raise heart rate, challenge muscular endurance, and provide full-body movement through flexion, extension, weight-bearing, and transitional control.

That makes it especially relevant in programs aimed at general vitality, weight management, and cardiorespiratory conditioning. The safest public claim is that it can contribute to exercise volume and mobility when matched to the student's capacity.

### Joint mobilisation — comprehensive therapeutic mechanism

Every major synovial joint in the body is taken through its full range of motion in one round of Surya Namaskar:
- **Spinal joints**: full extension and full flexion with axial loading
- **Hip joints**: full extension (positions 3, 10), full flexion (positions 2, 5, 9, 11)
- **Knee joints**: full extension (positions 2, 8, 11) and flexion (positions 4, 5, 6)
- **Shoulder joints**: full elevation and extension (positions 1, 7, 12), compression (positions 4, 6)
- **Ankle joints**: dorsiflexion and plantar flexion across multiple positions
- **Wrist joints**: full dorsiflexion loading (positions 4, 6, 7, 8)

This systematic joint mobilisation stimulates synovial fluid production, cartilage nutrition, ligament tensile strength, and joint proprioception — explaining the therapeutic value in Arthritis and joint-stiffness presentations.

### Metabolic support

Like other moderate physical activity, repeated Surya Namaskar can contribute to energy expenditure, muscular work, and exercise-related glucose use. That makes it reasonable to include in broader metabolic-support programs, but direct claims about pancreatic stimulation or specific endocrine effects should be avoided unless stronger evidence emerges.

## 4. Kosha Level

**Primary:** Annamaya Kosha — comprehensive full-body joint mobilisation; cardiovascular conditioning; abdominal organ compression and release; posterior and anterior spinal alternating strengthening.

**Secondary:** Pranamaya Kosha — the synchronised breath and movement pattern makes Surya Namaskar an intensive pranayama practice; each position links to a specific breath phase, creating a systematic expansion and compression of the entire pranic field; the 12 mantra-linked positions represent a complete cycle of solar energy through the body from sunrise (position 1) to full illumination and return (position 12).

**Tertiary:** Manomaya Kosha — the mantra, the solar devotion, and the focused breath-synchronised movement of Surya Namaskar engage the emotional-mental (bhakti) dimension; it is simultaneously asana, pranayama, and a form of moving meditation.

## 6. Contraindications

- **Severe hypertension (uncontrolled)**: the strenuous aerobic demand and the inverted positions in forward bends are contraindicated
- **Acute back pain / disc prolapse**: avoid the dynamic loading of spinal extension and flexion in active disc herniation
- **Cardiac decompensation**: the aerobic demand is equivalent to moderate exercise; not appropriate in acute cardiac insufficiency
- **Wrist pain (severe)**: the plank and prostration positions load the wrists in full dorsiflexion; modify with fists or forearm support
- **Pregnancy (second trimester onward)**: the progressive abdominal compression and prone positions are unsuitable; prenatal yoga substitutions recommended

## 7. Variations & Progressions

**Beginner (3 rounds, slow pace)**: Learn each position separately before linking them in sequence. Begin with 3 slow rounds (3–4 minutes), focusing on correct breathing synchronisation.

**Standard therapeutic dose (IAYT)**: 3–6 rounds. Maintains all benefits with manageable exertion for clinical populations.

**Obesity protocol progressive target**: Build from 3 rounds over weeks to 12–24 rounds (20–30 minutes of sustained Surya Namaskar practice), at which point the cardiovascular conditioning effect becomes therapeutically significant.

**10-position variation**: Omit positions 5 and 9 (Śaśāṅkāsana); suitable when knee flexion is restricted or in dynamic flow contexts requiring higher pace.

**Mantra-integrated practice**: Chanting the full Bīja mantra and solar name at the start of each round adds the manomaya (mental-emotional) and sound resonance dimensions, elevating the practice from physical exercise to integrated yoga.
