# Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose)

*Bhujanga* (Sanskrit) — serpent, cobra. The signature prone backbend of classical hatha yoga and the foundational prone extension in all eight IAYT therapeutic protocols. The posture draws on the image of a cobra raising its hood — upper body lifted by spinal strength, lower body grounded and still.

**Evidence note:** Bhujangasana is well represented in the S-VYASA teaching materials and in back-pain-oriented yoga programs, but direct clinical evidence for **Bhujangasana alone** is limited. Most external evidence concerns multi-component yoga interventions, so the indication statements below mainly reflect source-based therapeutic usage rather than pose-only proof.

---

## 1. Technique

**Starting position (Sthiti):** Prone (Makarasana). Lie face-down with legs together, forehead resting on the floor.

**Practice:**
1. Place the palms flat on the floor beside the lower chest, fingers pointing forward, at the level of the last rib. Keep the elbows close together, drawing them toward each other — this is the key alignment cue; the hands are placed to *guide* the lift, not to push it.
2. The least pressure should be on the palms. The emphasis is on spinal muscular work, not arm-pressing.
3. Inhale slowly and arch the dorsal (thoracic) spine and neck backward, peeling the chest off the floor vertebra by vertebra. The body below the navel remains in contact with the ground throughout.
4. The head follows the spine into extension — gaze forward and slightly upward.
5. Maintain the posture for approximately 1 minute with normal breathing.
6. Exhale slowly and lower the trunk back to the floor. Rest in Makarasana.

**Breathing:** Inhale into the lift; breathe normally throughout the hold; exhale on release.

**Duration/rounds:** 1 minute hold; 1–2 rounds per session.

## 2. Anatomical Activation

### Primary muscles engaged
- **Erector spinae** (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis): primary extensors of the thoracic and lumbar spine; they produce and sustain the backbend
- **Multifidus**: deep segmental stabiliser that refines vertebral alignment during extension
- **Rhomboids and middle trapezius**: retract the scapulae, opening the chest and preventing shoulder rounding
- **Posterior deltoids**: assist in drawing the elbows close, maintaining the thoracic opening
- **Gluteus maximus and hamstrings**: isometrically active to stabilise the pelvis and prevent lumbar compression
- **Deep neck extensors** (semispinalis capitis, splenius capitis): extend the cervical spine and hold the head position

### Organs and structures affected
- **Anterior thoracic contents** — the thoracic cage is lifted and expanded; the lungs and pleural cavities receive a passive stretch
- **Abdominal viscera** — gentle compression against the floor stimulates abdominal organs including liver, stomach, and small intestine
- **Adrenal region** — some IAYT materials describe prone backbends as stimulating or energising, but this should be read as a source-based interpretation rather than a directly established adrenal effect

### Spinal structures
Thoracic facet joints move into extension and the anterior trunk is stretched. In practical terms, the posture is used to train extension capacity, chest opening, and posterior-chain endurance.

## 3. Physiological Effects

### Dorsal spinal rehabilitation — the main source-based structural rationale

The thoracic spine is the most commonly hyperkyphotic region in sedentary adults. Bhujangasana directly reverses this pattern by concentrically loading the thoracic extensors and eccentrically stretching the anterior chest structures (pectorals, anterior intercostals, rectus abdominis). Over repeated practice:

1. The erector spinae and multifidus work to hold the lifted position, which is why the posture is used for extension strength and endurance.
2. The movement reverses prolonged flexion and can help restore comfort in people who are habitually rounded through the thoracic spine.
3. Any disc or nerve-root effect should be treated cautiously and individually; the posture can help some people and aggravate others depending on the pain pattern.

### Respiratory benefit — the thoracic expansion mechanism

The thoracic expansion in Bhujangasana increases the anterior-posterior diameter of the chest. This:
- Stretches the respiratory diaphragm's central tendon attachments via the elevated chest
- Passively increases lung volume by expanding the thoracic cage
- Stretches the bronchial tree, which is supported by cartilaginous rings but can accumulate secretions in inflammatory conditions

In respiratory-oriented source programs, Bhujangasana is used as a chest-opening posture that can encourage fuller breathing and improve the subjective sense of thoracic expansion. That is a more defensible public claim than asserting a direct bronchodilating mechanism.

### Abdominal toning mechanism

With the body below the navel grounded, the rectus abdominis and obliques are under passive tension as the spine extends over them. Over repeated practice, this can contribute to abdominal wall engagement and postural balance, but it should not be marketed as a direct fat-reduction technique.

### Autonomic effect

Many practitioners experience the posture as both energising and opening. The exact autonomic mechanism is not established well enough to make a stronger public claim.

## 4. Kosha Level

**Primary:** Annamaya Kosha — direct mechanical action on the dorsal spine, thoracic extensors, and anterior abdominal wall.

**Secondary:** Pranamaya Kosha — thoracic expansion improves *prana vayu* (inward-moving vital energy) circulation; the opening of the chest at the anahata chakra region is associated with improved respiratory capacity and emotional resilience.

## 6. Contraindications

- Recent abdominal surgery — avoid for a minimum of 2 months; the prone position and spinal extension place tension on anterior abdominal healing tissues
- Cervical spondylosis — proceed with caution; limit the cervical extension range, keep the chin slightly tucked if hyperextension is painful
- Pregnancy (second trimester onward) — prone position not suitable
- Acute lumbar disc prolapse — consult before practice; extension may centralise symptoms in some disc patterns but aggravate others
- Uncontrolled hypertension or severe pain flare — avoid unsupervised full-version practice; the source materials are not fully consistent here, so clinician and therapist judgment matters

## 7. Variations & Progressions

**Beginner (Ardha Bhujangasana — Half Cobra):** Rise only to the forearm level (Sphinx position). This reduces the range of extension and is appropriate during early stages of rehabilitation or for those with thoracic stiffness.

**Standard (as described):** Palms at the last rib level, elbows close, lift to comfortable chest height with minimal hand pressure.

**Intermediate:** Lift the hands slightly off the floor for 3–5 breaths to ensure the lift is entirely muscular, not arm-driven. This dramatically increases the demand on the erector spinae.

**Progression:** [Dhanurasana](P0084-dhanurasana.md) — Bow Pose — adds leg engagement to the prone extension, creating a full spinal arc and introducing abdominal rocking which increases the therapeutic pressure on digestive organs.
