# Bhramari Pranayama (Humming Bee Breath)

*Bhramara* (Sanskrit) — the Indian black bee; *Pranayama* — regulation of the life force through breath. Bhramari derives its name from the humming sound produced during exhalation, which resembles the drone of a bee. The central experience of the practice is a long, steady humming exhalation with inward attention.

Bhramari is often paired with Nadi Suddhi as a calming pranayama in the IAYT system.

**Evidence note:** External evidence for Bhramari itself is still relatively limited and comes mostly from small physiological studies, related humming research, and broader yoga-program outcomes. Public-facing claims should stay focused on breathing pace, calming, and possible autonomic support rather than disease-specific certainty.

## 1. Technique

**Preparatory practices:**

**M-kara practice** (preparation for Bhramari):
- Close the lips gently (teeth slightly apart inside). Tongue rests behind lower teeth.
- Hum the sound 'MM' on any comfortable exhale — a buzzing nasal resonance.
- Feel vibration in the lips, teeth, jaw, sinuses, and skull.
- 3–5 rounds.

**N-kara practice:**
- Allow lips to part slightly. Hum 'NN' — a slightly higher resonance than M-kara.
- Observe the shift in vibration location — more anterior and nasal.
- 3–5 rounds.

These preparatory practices train the practitioner to produce and feel the vibration before it is incorporated into the full breathing pattern.

**Full Bhramari Pranayama:**

**Starting position (Sthiti):** Sit in any meditative posture — [Vajrasana](P0154-vajrasana.md), [Padmasana](P0166-padmasana.md), [Sukhasana](P0167-sukhasana.md), or chair sitting. Spine erect, face relaxed, eyes gently closed.

**Hand position — basic:** Place both thumbs gently on the tragus (the small cartilaginous flap at the entrance of the ear canal) or on the ear lobe. This is a gentle plug, not a forceful seal — the aim is to reduce external sound and amplify internal resonance.

**Hand position — Shanmukhi Mudra (advanced):** The six-opening mudra. Thumbs on ears; index fingers lightly on closed eyelids; middle fingers on the sides of the nose (not blocking nostrils); ring fingers above the upper lip; little fingers below the lower lip. This seals all facial openings, intensifying the vibrational experience. (Shanmukhi is introduced progressively after basic Bhramari is established.)

**The practice:**
1. Inhale fully through both nostrils — a smooth, deep inhalation. Feel the lungs fill.
2. On the exhalation, close the mouth gently (teeth slightly apart). With the vocal cords partially adducted (throat slightly constricted, as in the Ujjayi position), produce a continuous, smooth, deep humming sound — 'MMMM' — for the entire duration of the exhalation.
3. The sound should feel like it fills the head — resonating in the sinus cavities, the cranial vault, the throat, and the chest cavity.
4. At the end of the exhalation, release the thumb pressure and allow a natural passive inhalation.
5. This completes one round.

**Duration:** 5 rounds in standard IAYT protocols. Extended to 9–15 rounds in hypertension and anxiety protocols. The exhalation with humming is typically 6–10 seconds.

## 2. Anatomical Activation

### Primary structures engaged

**Vocal cords (vocal folds / glottis):** Partial adduction of the vocal cords produces the humming vibration. This is the acoustic source — the vibration originates here and propagates through the airway and surrounding bony structures.

**Paranasal sinuses** (maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid): The air-filled sinus cavities act as resonance chambers. The humming frequency (typically 130–150 Hz for M-kara) causes the sinus walls to vibrate — sympathetic resonance. This mechanical vibration is transmitted to the surrounding bone and brain tissue.

**Cranial vault and skull bones:** The cranial bones conduct vibrational energy from the sinuses throughout the skull — this is the anatomical basis of the traditional description that Bhramari "resonates through the entire head."

**Vagus nerve (Cranial Nerve X):** The slow exhalation during humming activates vagal afferents throughout the larynx, pharynx, and thorax. The laryngeal branch of the vagus nerve (superior and recurrent laryngeal nerves) is directly stimulated by the vocal cord vibration.

**Brainstem:** Vagal signals converge on the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) and the dorsal motor nucleus in the brainstem — the primary centres for parasympathetic outflow and cardiovascular regulation.

**Limbic system and hypothalamus:** The vibroacoustic stimulation reaches the limbic structures via the acoustic nerve and through bone conduction, affecting the amygdala (emotion-fear processing), hippocampus (stress memory), and hypothalamus (autonomic-endocrine regulation).

**Thyroid and parathyroid region:** Vibration is felt locally in the throat, but disease-specific gland claims should be treated cautiously unless directly supported by stronger evidence.

## 3. Physiological Effects

### Vibro-acoustic calming — the primary mechanism

The clearest public-facing description of Bhramari is that it combines a **long exhalation** with a **self-generated humming sound**. Those features may help reduce breathing rate, increase internal focus, and support a calmer autonomic state.

Humming research also suggests a rise in nasal nitric oxide during humming compared with quiet exhalation, which may be relevant for nasal airflow and sinus ventilation. That finding is interesting and worth keeping in the editorial record, but it should not be stretched into broad claims about hypertension, insomnia, headache, or endocrine regulation from Bhramari alone.

## 4. Kosha Level

**Primary:** Pranamaya Kosha — Bhramari directly regulates the pranic energy through breath and sound vibration. In yogic physiology, the humming sound creates a resonance that harmonises the flow of prana through the Sushumna nadi (the central channel), particularly through the Vishuddha Chakra (throat centre, associated with sound and communication) and Ajna Chakra (third eye, associated with mental clarity and intuition).

**Secondary:** Manomaya Kosha — the calming of mental fluctuations (*chitta vritti nirodha*) is one of the most immediate subjective effects of Bhramari. The self-generated internal sound acts as a practical *dharana* (concentration) object, drawing the mind inward.

**Tertiary:** Vijnanamaya Kosha — the meditative depth achievable during Bhramari, particularly with Shanmukhi Mudra, allows access to the discriminative intelligence layer. Extended practice transitions naturally into Pratyahara and Dharana.

## 5. Included In Source Protocols For

| Condition | Reasoning |
|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | Used as a calming, slow-exhalation practice within broader blood-pressure programs |
| **Anxiety / Stress Disorders** | Commonly used for immediate calming and inward focus |
| **Insomnia** | Often practiced before sleep as a settling breath |
| **Anger management** | The humming exhalation can interrupt escalation and slow the breath |
| **Migraine and tension headache** | Sometimes used when relaxation and sensory withdrawal are helpful |
| **Sinusitis and nasal congestion** | Humming may support sinus ventilation, though this is not the same as proving disease treatment |
| **Voice disorders / vocal hoarseness** | Gentle humming can be used as a vocal-awareness exercise when appropriate |
| **Tinnitus** | Sometimes used cautiously as a sound-attention practice under guidance |
| **Depression** | Included when calming, inward-attention, and breath regulation are part of the wider therapeutic plan |

## 6. Contraindications

- **Active ear infection (otitis media/externa)** — thumb pressure on the ear and increased internal ear vibration are contraindicated during acute infection. Practice without ear closure until infection resolves.
- **Perforated eardrum** — any significant alteration in ear canal pressure is contraindicated. Practise without thumb placement on tragus.
- **Severe sinus congestion with headache** — if sinuses are acutely inflamed and blocked, the vibration may increase pain. Clear passages with Jala Neti first.
- **Epilepsy** — the altered brainwave state induced by extended Bhramari (deep theta) can potentially lower the seizure threshold in susceptible individuals. Practice under supervision only, short duration.
- No contraindication for the basic practice without Shanmukhi Mudra in virtually all other conditions, including pregnancy.

## 7. Variations & Progressions

**M-kara and N-kara only:** The preparatory humming practices without full breath control — suitable for patients with severe breathlessness or anxiety who cannot manage a full pranayama cycle. 3–5 rounds of simple humming on any comfortable exhale.

**Without ear closure:** Standard Bhramari without thumb placement — the vibrational benefit is retained; the internal resonance amplification is reduced. Appropriate for patients with ear conditions.

**With Shanmukhi Mudra:** The full six-opening seal intensifies the internal vibrational experience. Introduced after basic Bhramari is established. Appropriate for advanced practitioners and those in meditation-focused protocols.

**Extended rounds:** 15–20 rounds for insomnia treatment (practised lying in Savasana before sleep). 9–15 rounds in the hypertension IAYT protocol.

**With visualisation:** Combined with the visualisation of blue light or a full moon at the Ajna Chakra — intensifies the calming and concentrative effect. Used in anxiety and depression protocols.

**Brahmari in Savasana:** Practised lying down with a soft pillow under the head — for patients who cannot sit, for pre-sleep use, or in fatigue-based conditions. The bone conduction of vibration through the cranium is actually enhanced when the head rests on a surface.
