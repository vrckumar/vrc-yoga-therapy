# Mood Disorders (Depression & Anxiety)

## 1. Definition & Classification

Mood disorders are a broad category of mental health conditions characterised by persistent disturbances in emotional state (mood) that are severe enough to impair daily functioning, relationships, and quality of life. They include all forms of clinical depression and anxiety disorders, and are sometimes collectively called **affective disorders**. The term "mood" encompasses the sustained subjective emotional colouring of experience — separate from momentary emotional reactions.

Yoga therapy treats mood disorders as the most direct expression of **Manomaya Kosha** imbalance — the six enemies (*Kama* — intense desire, *Krodha* — anger, *Lobha* — miserliness, *Moha* — infatuation, *Mada* — arrogance, *Matsarya* — jealousy) manifest as excessive speed of thought, emotional reactivity, and loss of inner mastery. These are not merely "mental" problems in yoga therapy — they are understood as the root pathology from which all other Adhija Vyadhis (psychosomatic diseases) eventually develop.

**Evidence note:** In the S-VYASA source corpus, anxiety and depression are framed primarily through the Pancha Kosha model, with breath regulation, movement, meditation, rest practices, and counselling used together. External trials and reviews suggest that yoga-based programs can be helpful **adjuncts** for some people with depression, anxiety, or stress symptoms, but they do not replace psychiatric assessment, psychotherapy, medication when indicated, or crisis care.

### Classification of Mood Disorders

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD):**
A period of at least two weeks of depressed or irritable mood or markedly diminished interest in all usual activities, accompanied by five or more symptoms from the diagnostic cluster (see Section 3). Major depression is episodic and can be severe enough to require hospitalisation.

**Dysthymia (Dysthymic Disorder / Persistent Depressive Disorder):**
A chronic, low-grade depressed or irritable mood lasting at least one year (children: one year; adults: two years). Less severe than MDD but more persistent; significantly impairs quality of life.

**Bipolar Disorder (Manic-Depressive Illness):**
At least one episode of mania (persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with inflated self-esteem, reduced need for sleep, pressured speech, racing thoughts, risky behaviour) alongside depressive episodes. Requires mood stabilisers; yoga therapy is an important adjunct.

**Anxiety Disorders (in the yoga therapy framework):**
Anxiety is treated as the Pranamaya-Manomaya expression of hypersensitivity — the excessive speed of mind in anticipatory mode. Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD), Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, and Phobias all share the core mechanism of hyperactivated sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis dysregulation.

**Comorbidity:**
Depression and anxiety co-occur in more than 60% of patients. Yoga therapy's integrative approach simultaneously addresses both through the same Pancha Kosha protocol, adjusting the emphasis of specific practices.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology

### Neurobiological Substrate

**Limbic System:**
The emotional brain — comprising the amygdala, hippocampus, hypothalamus, anterior cingulate cortex, and interconnected structures. The amygdala is the alarm centre: it detects threat (real or imagined) and triggers the stress response. In depression and anxiety, the amygdala is chronically hyperactive — responding to non-threatening stimuli as if they were dangerous, and sustaining the stress response long after the triggering event.

**Prefrontal Cortex (PFC):**
The rational, executive brain that modulates emotional reactivity from the amygdala. In healthy functioning, the PFC dampens amygdala activation — applying context, perspective, and voluntary regulation. In depression and chronic anxiety, PFC activity is reduced and its inhibitory control over the amygdala is weakened, allowing emotional reactivity to dominate.

**HPA Axis Hyperreactivity:**
The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is the body's primary stress-response system:
- **Hypothalamus** detects stress signals → releases Corticotropin-Releasing Hormone (CRH)
- **Pituitary** receives CRH → releases Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH)
- **Adrenal cortex** receives ACTH → releases **cortisol**

In depression and anxiety, this axis is chronically hyperactivated — cortisol levels remain elevated even in the absence of acute stress. Chronically elevated cortisol:
- Suppresses hippocampal neurogenesis (causing cognitive impairment and memory problems)
- Reduces serotonin synthesis
- Increases inflammatory cytokines
- Creates insulin resistance
- Maintains sympathetic dominance

**Neurotransmitter Imbalances:**
- **Serotonin (5-HT):** Reduced synthesis and receptor sensitivity → depressed mood, sleep disturbance, anxiety
- **Dopamine:** Reduced activity → anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), lack of motivation, fatigue
- **Norepinephrine:** Dysregulated → energy fluctuations, concentration impairment
- **GABA:** Reduced inhibitory tone → anxiety, heightened reactivity
- **Endorphins:** Reduced production → absence of the "feel-good" neurotransmitters

**Yogic model (Gita):** The source tradition presents a parallel description of distress as a progression from *Chinta* (worry) → *Udvega* (excessive speed of emotions) → loss of *Viveka* (discriminative intelligence) → *Sammoha* (confusion/delusion) → *Smriti Bhramsha* (memory loss) → *Buddhi Nasha* (intellect disruption) → *Pranasha* (collapse). This is best read as a conceptual parallel rather than a one-to-one biomedical map.

## 3. Causes & Symptoms

### Causes

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Biogenic amine imbalance | Reduced serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine; HPA axis hyperreactivity |
| Genetic predisposition | Multifactorial inheritance; family history increases risk 2–3× |
| Chronic psychosocial stress | Life events, occupational stress, relationship conflict |
| Gender | Depression twice as common in women (12% vs. 6.6% in men) |
| Medical conditions | Cancer, hypothyroidism, Parkinson's, cardiac disease, chronic pain — all associated |
| Substance use | Alcohol, recreational drugs → serotonin dysregulation |
| Psychological factors | Perfectionism, excessive speed of mind, hypersensitivity (yogic root causes) |

### Symptoms

**Depressive symptoms:**
- Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or emptiness
- Loss of interest or pleasure in previously enjoyed activities (anhedonia)
- Low self-esteem; excessive guilt; feeling worthless
- Sleep disturbances — insomnia or hypersomnia
- Changes in appetite — overeating or loss of appetite; weight changes
- Fatigue; decreased energy; slowed thinking and movement
- Difficulty concentrating, making decisions
- Suicidal ideation or behaviour (serious red flag — requires immediate medical evaluation)
- Somatic complaints: headache, stomach ache, musculoskeletal pain

**Anxiety symptoms:**
- Excessive worry, dread, or apprehension
- Restlessness, feeling "on edge"
- Difficulty concentrating; mind going blank
- Irritability, hostility
- Muscle tension; psychosomatic physical symptoms
- Sleep difficulty (difficulty falling asleep due to racing thoughts)
- Panic attacks (sudden onset of intense fear with physical symptoms: palpitation, breathlessness, chest tightness, sweating, derealization)
- Avoidance behaviours; phobias (specific, social, or agoraphobic)

## 4. Conventional Treatment

### Diagnosis

Mood disorders are diagnosed by psychiatrists or mental health professionals through comprehensive psychiatric evaluation (clinical interview, standardised rating scales). No biological test currently confirms the diagnosis.

**Rating scales:** PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety), HAM-D (Hamilton Depression Rating Scale), HADS (Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale), BDI (Beck Depression Inventory).

### Medical Management

**Antidepressants:**
| Class | Drugs | Target |
|---|---|---|
| SSRIs (first-line) | Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Escitalopram | Increase synaptic serotonin |
| SNRIs | Venlafaxine, Duloxetine | Increase serotonin + norepinephrine |
| TCAs | Amitriptyline, Imipramine | Older; multiple receptor effects |
| MAOIs | Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine | Prevent serotonin/norepinephrine breakdown |

**Mood stabilisers (Bipolar):** Lithium, Valproate, Lamotrigine.

**Anxiolytics:** Benzodiazepines (short-term only); Buspirone; Beta-blockers (performance anxiety); SSRIs (first-line for chronic anxiety).

**Psychotherapy:** Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (CBT) — most evidence-based; changes distorted thought patterns. Interpersonal Therapy (IPT). When combined with antidepressants, therapy produces superior outcomes to either alone.

## 5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

### Pancha Kosha Analysis

Mood disorders are paradigmatic Manomaya Kosha diseases. In the yogic model, the mind's excessive speed (*Vega*) — the unceasing loop of worried, reactive, self-critical thoughts — is the root of both depression (speed that turns inward and exhausts) and anxiety (speed that turns outward and escalates). The IAYT addresses this at every level, with particular emphasis on the Manomaya Kosha.

### Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

**Goal:** Use movement, breath, and posture to improve self-regulation, sleep readiness, embodied steadiness, and tolerance for stillness.

- **Dynamic exercises and Sakti Vikasaka:** Regular physical activity is one of the strongest non-pharmacological supports for mood symptoms. Within yoga therapy, dynamic movement is used to reduce stagnation, improve sleep drive, and build manageable activation.
- **Balancing postures:** Postures requiring attention and steadiness can help train concentration, grounding, and confidence.
- **Asanas for depression:** Active, energising, forward-heart-opening postures — Bhujangasana, Ustrasana, Virabhadrasana — activate sympathetic tone constructively, counteracting the withdrawn, collapsed physical posture of depression.
- **Asanas for anxiety:** Grounding, forward-bending, cooling postures — [Paschimottanasana](../practices/P0086-paschimottanasana.md), [Sasankasana](../practices/P0097-sasankasana.md), Balasana — calm excess sympathetic activation.
- **Satvik diet:** Adequate and wholesome diet, neither rajasic (stimulating) nor tamasic (dulling). Rajasic foods trigger brain hyperactivity; tamasic foods promote lethargy. Satvik diet supports balanced neurotransmitter function.

### Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

**Goal:** Regulate breathing pace, reduce over-arousal, and build a reliable bridge between body state and mental state.

The breath is the bridge between mind and body — an agitated mind produces shallow, irregular breathing; a calm breath produces a calmer mind. Pranayama interventions differ depending on whether depression or anxiety predominates.

**For Depression (insufficient sympathetic/energetic tone):**
- **Surya Anuloma Viloma (Right Nostril Breathing):** Traditionally used when low energy, dullness, or heaviness predominate.
- **[Kapalabhati](../practices/P0072-kapalabhati.md):** Used selectively as an energising practice in low-energy states, but it should be introduced cautiously because fast breathing can be too activating for some people.
- **Bhastrika (Bellows Breathing):** A stimulating practice reserved for appropriate students and not a default choice in anxious or unstable states.

**For Anxiety (excess sympathetic/hyperactive tone):**
- **Chandra Anuloma Viloma (Left Nostril Breathing):** Traditionally used as a cooling and calming practice in anxiety, panic, and hyperreactivity.
- **[Nadi Suddhi](../practices/P0069-nadi-suddhi.md):** Often used as the balancing pranayama because slow alternate-nostril breathing can support steadier attention and calmer breathing.
- **Sitali / Sitkari:** Cooling pranayamas that may help reduce subjective heat and agitation.
- **Extended exhalation (1:2 ratio):** Longer exhalation is often calming and is a practical first-line tool for anxious breathing patterns.

**For both:**
- **[Bhramari](../practices/P0071-bhramari.md) (Humming Bee Breath):** Humming exhalation can provide an immediate auditory-breath anchor during panic, agitation, or emotional overwhelm.
- **[Nadanusandhana](../practices/P0164-nadanusandana.md):** The progression from Bhramari through deeper sound resonance practices; cultivates inner sound awareness that naturally slows thought speed.

### Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

**Goal:** Slow the excessive speed of the reactive mind; develop mastery over the emotional loops of depression and anxiety; rebuild the connection to inner stillness.

This is the primary level of intervention for mood disorders.

- **[Cyclic Meditation](../practices/P0121-cyclic-meditation.md):** S-VYASA studies and related yoga-rest literature suggest that structured movement-rest cycles may help reduce stress, improve sleep, and reduce self-reported anxiety in some groups.
- **Yoga Nidra (NSDR — Non-Sleep Deep Rest):** May improve rest, sleep quality, and down-regulation, especially in people who feel mentally overdriven or physically exhausted.
- **Mind Sound Resonance Technique (MSRT):** Mantra-based progressive relaxation using the four seed sounds (AAA, UUU, MMM, OM) — systematically reverberates through the physical, vital, and mental bodies, producing deep relaxation with simultaneous cognitive clarity.
- **[OM Meditation](../practices/P0119-om-meditation.md) with [Nadanusandhana](../practices/P0164-nadanusandana.md):** Training in focussing and defocusing through the three aspects of OM — *Sravana* (hearing), *Manana* (recollecting), *Nididhyasana* (dwelling in the state of inner silence). The progressive development of a stable, slow inner mind is the direct antidote to the excessive speed that is the yogic root of mood disorders.
- **Devotional sessions (bhajans, chants):** Group devotional practice provides emotional regulation through collective resonance — literally co-regulating the nervous system. The experience of being held in a larger awareness relieves the isolation of depression.
- **Japa (silent mantra repetition):** Provides a simple cognitive anchor — the mantra — that interrupts rumination loops and slowly redirects the mental stream.
- **VISAK technique:** For anxiety or phobic reactivity, the source materials describe a focus-defocus method for gradually changing the relationship to feared sensations or situations.
- **Dharana → Dhyana progression:** Beginning with focused attention (Dharana) on a single object (breath, mantra, or image) and progressing to effortless awareness (Dhyana) — the progressive development of mental concentration counteracts the scattered, fragmented thinking of anxiety and the withdrawn, repetitive thinking of depression.

### Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

**Goal:** Correct the notional errors — about happiness, identity, worth, and security — that sustain chronic mood disorders.

The intellectual layer of yoga therapy for mood disorders involves deep examination of the beliefs that drive emotional reactivity. Key notional corrections:
- The notion that self-worth depends on achievement, approval, or external conditions → corrected through *Atmabodha* (self-knowledge)
- The notion that happiness comes from acquiring objects, relationships, or status → corrected through *Viveka* (discrimination — the recognition that lasting happiness is found within)
- The notion that one must fight the world to survive → corrected through Karma Yoga and Bhakti Yoga

Lectures, counselling, and satsang provide the intellectual scaffolding within which these corrections can occur. Working at Vijnanamaya is described as "burning the attachments and obsessions" that are the cognitive fuel for emotional dysregulation.

### Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

**Goal:** Reconnect with the inherent inner bliss that is not conditional on external circumstances — the deepest healing for mood disorders.

Happy Assembly (group play and celebration) provides a concrete experience of joy independent of circumstances — a direct antidote to anhedonia. The philosophical recognition that "each soul is potentially divine" — that inner bliss is the natural state obscured by mental agitation — provides the deepest motivation for sustained practice. Karma Yoga (acting without craving for specific outcomes) dissolves the root source of reactive mood swings.

## 7. Contraindications & Cautions

- **Active suicidal ideation:** Yoga therapy is an adjunct to — not a replacement for — psychiatric treatment. Active suicidality requires immediate medical/psychiatric intervention. Yoga therapy continues alongside, not instead of, pharmacological treatment in this scenario.
- **Bipolar disorder — manic phase:** Strong stimulating practices (Bhastrika, vigorous Surya Namaskara, long Surya Anuloma Viloma) should be avoided in the manic phase — they may escalate mania. Emphasise grounding, cooling, and calming practices during manic episodes.
- **Panic disorder:** Begin slowly with abdominal breathing — breath awareness practice initially may increase anxiety if introduced without proper support. Build rapport and trust before commencing pranayama.
- **Kapalabhati:** Avoid in hypertension, pregnancy, recent abdominal surgery, severe anxiety with hyperventilation tendency.
- **Inversions:** Caution in patients with severe anxiety and panic disorder — inversions can trigger derealization in vulnerable patients. Introduce gradually after stabilisation.
- **Antidepressant medication:** Continue prescribed medications; do not advise patients to reduce medication without psychiatric consultation.
- **Isolation:** Group practice is therapeutically important in mood disorders — do not reduce to only individual home practice.

## 8. Evidence & Research Parameters

Research parameters commonly used when studying yoga-based interventions for mood symptoms:

| Parameter | Measure | Direction with Yoga |
|---|---|---|
| PHQ-9 (depression) | 0–27 score | ↓ Reduction expected |
| GAD-7 (anxiety) | 0–21 score | ↓ Reduction expected |
| HADS (Hospital Anxiety & Depression Scale) | 0–42 (two subscales) | ↓ Reduction expected |
| BDI (Beck Depression Inventory) | 0–63 score | ↓ Reduction expected |
| Cortisol (morning) | nmol/L | ↓ Reduction with meditation component |
| HRV (Heart Rate Variability) | Parasympathetic tone (LF/HF ratio) | ↑ Improvement expected |
| Sleep quality (PSQI) | 0–21 | ↓ Improvement (lower score = better sleep) |
| Quality of life (WHO-QoL) | Validated scale | ↑ Improvement expected |
| Cognitive function (attention, memory) | Neuropsychological tests | ↑ Improvement |
| BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) | ng/mL | ↑ Increase with yoga and exercise |
| Serotonin (serum) | ng/mL | ↑ Increase expected |

S-VYASA program-level studies and external trials suggest that yoga-based programs may improve anxiety, depression, sleep, and perceived stress in some populations. The evidence is encouraging but heterogeneous, so public-facing claims should stay focused on adjunctive support rather than guaranteed symptom reversal or any single biological mechanism.
