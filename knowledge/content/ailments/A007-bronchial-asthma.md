# Bronchial Asthma

## 1. Definition & Classification

Bronchial asthma (from Greek *asthma* — panting, gasping) is a condition characterised by recurrent attacks of difficulty in exhalation due to widespread, reversible narrowing of the airways in the lungs. The narrowing varies in severity over short time periods, either spontaneously or in response to treatment. Two defining features distinguish it from other causes of breathlessness: **bronchial hyperreactivity** — an exaggerated airway response to stimuli that would not affect normal persons — and **reversibility** — the airway obstruction reverses, at least partially, with bronchodilator therapy or spontaneously.

Underlying all asthmatic episodes is airway **inflammation**, which causes mucus accumulation, mucosal oedema, and contraction of bronchial smooth muscle — the triad that produces the characteristic wheeze, chest tightness, and breathlessness.

**Evidence note:** In the S-VYASA source corpus, asthma is presented through an IAYT model that emphasises breath regulation, relaxation, emotional steadiness, and progressive self-mastery. External reviews support yoga-based programs as an **adjunct** to standard asthma care for symptom control, quality of life, and some lung-function measures, but they do not support replacing inhalers, trigger management, or medical follow-up.

### Classification

**Seasonal asthma** — attacks occur during specific weeks or months in consecutive years (associated with pollen, fungal spores, specific weather patterns); patient is asthma-free between seasons.

**Perennial asthma** — symptoms arise daily or near-daily throughout the year, without clear seasonal variation. Usually associated with indoor allergens (house dust mite, animal dander) or non-specific irritants.

**Irregular (intermittent) asthma** — episodes at irregular intervals throughout the year, with complete remission of varying duration (weeks to months) in between.

**Status asthmaticus** — a severe, prolonged asthmatic attack lasting more than 24 hours despite repeated bronchodilator and oral medication. Signs of dehydration and prolonged hypoxia may be present. A medical emergency requiring hospitalisation. More common in steroid-dependent asthmatics.

## 2. Anatomy & Physiology

### The Respiratory Passages

Air enters through the nose (filtering, warming, humidifying incoming air via turbinate bones and ciliated mucosa), descends through the pharynx and larynx into the **trachea** — a 10 cm tube maintained patent by incomplete cartilage rings. The trachea bifurcates into the right and left **bronchi**, which subdivide progressively into smaller bronchi, then **bronchioles**, and finally enter the **alveoli** — the terminal air sacs where gas exchange occurs.

The critical anatomical feature for asthma understanding is the **bronchial wall**: beneath the ciliated mucosal lining lies a distinct layer of **circular smooth muscle**, running the entire length of the bronchial tree from large bronchi to small bronchioles. Contraction of this muscle layer — bronchoconstriction — narrows the airway lumen. Relaxation — bronchodilation — opens it. The calibre of these muscles is dynamically regulated by the autonomic nervous system:

- **Sympathetic activation** → bronchodilation (beta-2 adrenergic receptors on smooth muscle → cAMP increase → muscle relaxation)
- **Parasympathetic activation (vagus nerve)** → bronchoconstriction (muscarinic receptors → cGMP increase → muscle contraction)

**Mast cells** embedded in the bronchial mucosa and submucosa store mediators — histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins — that are released when IgE-sensitised mast cells encounter their specific allergens, causing both immediate bronchoconstriction and the subsequent inflammatory cascade.

### Lung Function Measures Relevant to Asthma

| Measure | Definition | In Asthma |
|---|---|---|
| FVC (Forced Vital Capacity) | Max volume exhaled forcibly after full inhalation | Reduced in severe obstruction |
| FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 sec) | Volume exhaled in first second of FVC manoeuvre | Reduced (disproportionately) |
| FEV1/FVC ratio | Normally >80% in adults | Reduced (<70%) — characteristic of obstructive disease |
| PFR (Peak Flow Rate) | Highest flow rate during forced exhalation | Reduced; correlates with degree of obstruction |
| Slow Vital Capacity (SVC) | Volume exhaled slowly and completely | Greater than FVC in asthmatics (due to air trapping) |

Reversibility testing: if FEV1 increases by ≥15% after inhaling a bronchodilator, airway obstruction is classified as reversible, confirming asthma.

### The Autonomic Imbalance Hypothesis

The S-VYASA explanation gives importance to autonomic reactivity, especially the way stress, fear, rapid breathing, and trigger exposure can worsen bronchospasm in a susceptible person. In biomedical terms, asthma is better understood as a chronic inflammatory airway disorder in which autonomic tone, breathing pattern, and emotional stress can all influence symptom expression.

The older cGMP-cAMP framing can be read as a source-era attempt to describe bronchial tone regulation, but current public-facing wording should stay more cautious: yoga may help some patients by improving breathing efficiency, reducing panic around breathlessness, and supporting overall symptom control within a broader treatment plan.

## 3. Causes & Symptoms

### Trigger Factors

Asthma has no single cause. Genes carry the tendency (polygenic inheritance — multiple genes govern age of onset, degree of airway inflammation, medication response), and the environment provides the triggers:

**Allergens:**
- Pollen (tree, grass, weed — seasonal)
- House dust mite — *Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus* — lives in carpets, mattresses, and soft furnishings; the most common indoor trigger, particularly in children
- Animal dander — cats and horses are potent sensitisers
- Fungal spores

**Respiratory infections:** Viral upper respiratory infections (influenza, rhinovirus) reduce the threshold of vagal nerve endings in the bronchial mucosa, triggering bronchoconstriction even in non-asthmatic subjects. In asthmatics, viral infections commonly precipitate severe acute episodes.

**Physical exertion:** Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction results from airway cooling and drying during increased ventilation. Running in cold open air is a stronger trigger than swimming in a warm indoor pool.

**Environmental irritants:** Cigarette smoke, dusty environments, chemical fumes (hydrochloric acid, sulphur dioxide, hydrogen sulphide, plastic fumes), strong perfumes and odours, cold air.

**Occupational exposures:** Baker's flour, animal proteins (animal handlers, bee keepers), cotton dust (histamine-releasing substance), industrial chemicals — triggering IgE-mediated or direct irritant mechanisms.

**Food sensitivities:** Peanuts (acute IgE-mediated), dairy products or wheat (more chronic, non-dramatic response), sulphites (preservatives in wine, dried fruits).

**Psychological factors:** Emotional stress — excitement, grief, anxiety, suppressed anger — activates the vagus nerve through the limbic cortex → hypothalamus → autonomic pathway, directly inducing bronchoconstriction. Hyperventilation during psychological stress is itself a trigger (similar mechanism to exercise-induced asthma). Asthmatics tend toward psychological hypersensitivity, meticulous temperament, and repressed emotional expression — patterns that sustain the autonomic hyperreactivity.

### Signs and Symptoms

- Episodic breathlessness, characteristically worse on exhalation
- **Wheeze** — a polyphonic whistling sound on exhalation due to airflow through narrowed bronchi; the diagnostic hallmark
- Chest tightness, sense of constriction
- Nocturnal cough (classic presentation: cough worse between 2–4 am when cortisol, a natural bronchodilator, is at its nadir)
- Nasal allergy — sneezing, rhinorrhoea, blocked nose, itching of nose and ears
- Disturbed sleep, fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance
- Psychological burden: anxiety, depression, frustration, social withdrawal

### Symptoms of Acute Severe Episode (Status Asthmatics)

Respiratory rate >25/min, inability to complete sentences, cyanosis, dehydration, pulsus paradoxus — requires emergency medical management.

## 4. Conventional Treatment

### Preventive Measures

Reduce allergen exposure: regular vacuuming of carpets, encasing mattresses and pillows in allergen-proof covers, removing domestic pets from sleeping areas, maintaining bedroom temperature to inhibit dust mite proliferation, avoiding known food triggers.

### Pharmacological Management

**Relievers (used acutely):**
- Beta-2 agonists: Salbutamol, Terbutaline — inhaled short-acting bronchodilators; relax bronchial smooth muscle within minutes via sympathomimetic pathway (increase cAMP)
- Aminophylline — theophylline group; inhibits phosphodiesterase → raises cAMP → bronchodilation; IV route in emergencies

**Preventers (used regularly):**
- Inhaled corticosteroids (beclomethasone, fluticasone) — reduce airway inflammation; the mainstay of regular management in persistent asthma
- Cromoglycate (sodium cromoglycate) — stabilises mast cell membranes, preventing mediator release when antigen encounters IgE-bound mast cell
- Long-acting beta-2 agonists (salmeterol, formoterol) — sustained bronchodilation as add-on therapy
- Systemic corticosteroids (prednisolone) — reserved for acute exacerbations and severe refractory asthma; significant side effects with long-term use

**Antihistamines:** Neutralise histamine at bronchial and nasal mucosa; more relevant for upper airway allergy (rhinitis) than for bronchospasm.

**Delivery methods:** Inhalers (MDI, dry powder), nebulisers, oral tablets. Inhaled route preferred for bronchospasm — higher local concentration, fewer systemic side effects.

## 5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

### How Yoga Addresses Asthma — Layer by Layer

Asthma, in the IAYT model, is a condition in which emotional reactivity, breathing pattern, and autonomic imbalance can amplify respiratory difficulty. The disturbance is described as beginning at the Manomaya Kosha, influencing the Pranamaya Kosha through irregular or panic-driven breathing, and then expressing at the physical level as worsened airway reactivity.

### Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

**Goal:** Improve respiratory mechanics; desensitise the airway; develop voluntary mastery over the bronchial reflex.

**Yogasanas** for asthma emphasise chest-opening, thoracic extension, and diaphragmatic engagement. Bhujangasana (cobra) and Salabhasana expand the anterior thorax, counteracting the habitually contracted, rounded-shoulder posture of the chronic asthmatic who protects their chest during episodes. Sarvangasana and Matsyasana improve thoracic circulation and lymphatic drainage of the bronchial tissues. Vakrasana and Ardha Matsyendrasana apply spinal rotation that mobilises the thoracic spine and rib cage, improving chest wall compliance.

**Kriyas used in some source protocols:**
- **[Jala Neti](../practices/P0115-jala-neti.md)** — saline nasal cleansing used to reduce upper-airway irritation and improve nasal patency.
- **[Vamana Dhouti](../practices/P0117-vamana-dhauti.md) (Kunjal Kriya)** — included in some traditional therapeutic settings, but should be treated as a specialised supervised practice rather than a general public recommendation for asthma self-care.
- **[Kapalabhati](../practices/P0072-kapalabhati.md) (slow strokes)** — when used gently and only in stable patients, it may help awareness of abdominal breathing and respiratory muscle engagement.

**Loosening exercises** — forward/backward bending, side bending, Pavanamuktasana Kriya — mobilise the thoracic cage and spine, improving chest wall flexibility that is often reduced in long-standing asthma.

### Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

**Goal:** Improve breathing regulation, reduce over-breathing and panic, and support more stable autonomic balance.

The respiratory system is the most direct interface of the Pranamaya Kosha with the external world. Pranayama works here with maximum directness:

- **Surya Anuloma Viloma (right nostril breathing)** — traditionally used as a mildly energising breath practice.

- **Chandra Anuloma Viloma** — interleaved with Surya practices in some source protocols to balance activation and calming.

- **[Nadi Suddhi](../practices/P0069-nadi-suddhi.md)** — the balancing pranayama; slow alternate-nostril breathing may improve respiratory pacing and perceived control over breath.

- **[Vibhagiya Svasana](../practices/P0068-vibhagiya-svasana.md) (sectional breathing)** — abdominal, thoracic, and clavicular sections practiced sequentially. Corrects the habitual shallow upper-chest breathing of the anxious asthmatic, restoring full diaphragmatic excursion and the parasympathetic activation it provides.

- **Sitali / Sitkari / Sadanta** — cooling pranayamas used to encourage slower breathing and reduce subjective distress.

- **[Bhramari](../practices/P0071-bhramari.md)** — humming exhalation can lengthen the breath and reduce the panic that often accompanies breathlessness.

- **Ujjayi** — gentle glottic narrowing can provide a paced-breathing cue, though it should not be forced in anyone who feels air hunger.

### Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

**Goal:** Resolve the suppressed emotional conflicts and persistent anxiety that sustain vagal hyperreactivity; reduce the mental speed that generates the psychosomatic drive of asthmatic episodes.

The source model gives major importance to emotional hypersensitivity, anxiety, and conflict as amplifiers of symptoms. Public-facing wording should be more modest: stress and anxiety can worsen asthma control, but they do not explain all asthma.

- **[Deep Relaxation Technique (DRT)](../practices/P0123-drt.md)** — may reduce whole-body tension and help the practitioner feel less threatened by breath discomfort.

- **[Cyclic Meditation](../practices/P0121-cyclic-meditation.md) (OM Dhyana)** — used in the S-VYASA system as a structured practice for down-regulation, relaxation, and reduction of stress-linked symptom amplification.

- **Devotional sessions** — emotional regulation through the path of bhakti. Bhajans, prayers, and chants stabilise hypothalamic reactivity, reduce the emotional suppression that characterises asthmatic personality, and provide healthy channels for emotional expression that do not require suppression.

- **Yoga Nidra** — threshold-of-sleep deep rest systematically releases the deep neuromuscular holding patterns associated with chronic anxiety and respiratory guarding.

### Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

**Goal:** Resolve the psychological conflicts and wrong understanding that sustain the emotional volatility underlying asthmatic hyperreactivity.

Counselling and lectures help the asthmatic understand the precise mechanism by which their suppressed emotional conflicts activate the vagus nerve and trigger bronchospasm — not as a theoretical abstraction but as an observable process in their own body. This understanding transforms the patient from a passive recipient of attacks to an active practitioner who can observe the emotional precursors of an attack and intervene at the level of the Manomaya Kosha before the cascade reaches the bronchi.

The Upanishadic happiness analysis — demonstrating that inner silence is the substrate of happiness, not the external objects or relationships that the anxious mind craves — provides the philosophical foundation for a genuinely different emotional life.

### Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

**Goal:** Dissolve the repressed emotional backlog and the ego-bound personality that sustains it; experience the expansive, open inner state that is the antithesis of the contracted, bronchospastic body.

Karma Yoga practice teaches action in relaxation — doing one's duty fully and joyfully without the compulsive craving or aversion that generates emotional suppression. The happy assembly (community practice, joyful shared activity) serves an additional function in asthma: it provides a safe, non-threatening emotional environment where suppressed emotions can surface and be discharged in a positive group context, reducing the burden of unexpressed feeling that accumulates in the Manomaya Kosha.

## 7. Contraindications & Cautions

- **Active acute episode:** No asana practice during an acute bronchospastic attack. Slow sectional breathing, Bhramari, and DRT may help reduce panic and mild bronchospasm during early-stage attacks. Any patient with severe breathlessness requires immediate bronchodilator and medical attention.
- **Kapalabhati at speed:** Contraindicated in active bronchospasm — the rapid diaphragmatic effort can trigger reflex bronchoconstriction. Slow, gentle Kapalabhati strokes only, in stable patients.
- **Bhastrika:** Avoid — hyperventilation effect can trigger bronchospasm in asthma (same mechanism as exercise-induced asthma).
- **Inversions:** Use with caution. Sarvangasana is beneficial when practiced gently in a stable patient; contraindicated if there is upper respiratory infection, sinusitis, or uncontrolled severe asthma.
- **Jala Neti after ear surgery or with nasal polyps:** Jala Neti (water) is contraindicated; Danda Neti (catheter) may be used carefully if no nasal polyps are present.
- **Cold environments:** Practice in warm, dust-free environments. Cold air can trigger bronchospasm during breathing exercises.
- **Medication timing:** Patients should not stop or reduce prescribed inhalers without medical supervision. Yoga practices are adjunctive and may reduce medication requirements over time, but changes to medication should follow clinical assessment.
- **Pranayama breath retention:** Avoid Kumbhaka in active asthma — oxygen tension changes during breath retention may trigger or worsen bronchospasm.
- **Over-protective patterns:** Over-anxious parents or family members treating the asthmatic as fragile can become a secondary sustaining cause. The yoga therapy environment encourages confidence, mastery, and progressive challenge.

## 8. Evidence & Research Parameters

Research outcome measures commonly used when studying yoga-based asthma interventions:

| Parameter | Measurement Method | Expected Direction with Yoga |
|---|---|---|
| Peak Flow Rate (PFR) | Mini peak flow meter — best of 3 readings | ↑ Improvement; less diurnal variability |
| FVC (Forced Vital Capacity) | Spirometry | ↑ Improvement |
| FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume, 1 sec) | Spirometry | ↑ Improvement |
| FEV1/FVC ratio (%) | Spirometry (post-bronchodilator reversibility) | ↑ Improvement toward normal |
| Bronchial Lability Index (BLI) | Exercise provocation test: % Rise + % Fall of PFR | ↓ Reduction in bronchial hyperreactivity |
| Frequency of acute episodes | Patient diary | ↓ Reduction in episode frequency and severity |
| Medication use | Inhaler puff count / oral medication log | ↓ Reduction over 3–6 months |
| Anxiety / psychological scores | Standardised questionnaire | ↓ Reduction |
| Heart Rate Variability | Short-term HRV recording | ↑ Improvement (parasympathetic-sympathetic balance) |
| Quality of life score | Asthma-specific QoL questionnaire | ↑ Improvement |

**Exercise provocation testing protocol:** PFR recorded before exercise; patient performs standardised exercise until pulse reaches 160–180 bpm; PFR recorded at 1-minute intervals for 20 minutes post-exercise. % Rise (during exercise) and % Fall (after exercise) are calculated against baseline. BLI = % Rise + % Fall. A reduction in BLI with yoga intervention indicates reduced bronchial hyperreactivity.

S-VYASA program-level studies and external systematic reviews suggest that yoga-based programs may improve symptom control, quality of life, and some spirometric measures in asthma. The most defensible public claim is that yoga can be used as an adjunctive self-management support, especially where breath pacing, relaxation, and trigger awareness are emphasized.
